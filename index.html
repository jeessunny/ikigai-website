What Is Ikigai?

In Japanese, ikigai (生き甲斐) translates, roughly, to "that which makes life worth living." It is not a destination — it is the sensation of waking with intention. A convergence of meaning, competence, contribution, and sustenance that quietly structures a well-lived life.

"Only staying active will make you want to live a hundred years."

— A centenarian of Ogimi, Okinawa

García and Miralles spent time in Ogimi — the village with the world's highest concentration of centenarians — and found no one obsessing over purpose as an abstract ideal. Instead they found small rituals, deep craft, and relationships sustained across decades. Ikigai was not announced. It was lived.

"The people of Ogimi don't retire. They simply continue doing what they love."

— García & Miralles, Ikigai

The insight is that meaning does not arise from a single grand calling. It emerges at the intersection of four honest questions: What do you love? What are you good at? What does the world need? What can sustain you? Where these four circles overlap, something quiet and durable takes shape.

The Map

The Four Circles

"Where what you love, what you are good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for intersect — lies your direction."

Passion

What you love + What you are good at. Fulfilling but not yet fully purposeful.

Mission

What you love + What the world needs. Meaningful but perhaps not yet sustainable.

Profession

What you are good at + What you can be paid for. Stable but may feel hollow.

Vocation

What the world needs + What you can be paid for. Useful but not yet yours.

Question 1 of 5
01 / 05 — What You Love
What do you love so deeply that you lose track of time?

Think in specifics — not "art" but what kind, not "helping people" but in what way. The more honest the detail, the more meaningful the reflection.

02 / 05 — Your Strengths
What do people consistently come to you for?

Not what you think you should be good at — what others recognise in you, or what you do with an ease that surprises people around you.

03 / 05 — The World's Need
What is a problem or gap in the world that genuinely troubles you?

Not a trendy cause — something you notice and feel a quiet urgency about, even when others do not seem to see it.

04 / 05 — Livelihood
What work have people paid you for, or genuinely would?

Be grounded and honest. This is not about your dream income — it is about what others value enough to exchange something for.

05 / 05 — A Moment of Meaning
Describe a moment when you felt most alive, most yourself.

It does not have to be dramatic. A quiet afternoon, a conversation, a project — a moment when everything felt right. Describe what was happening.
